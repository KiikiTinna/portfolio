About Me

I am a Research Associate at Constructor University, specializing in Social Navigation and Human–Robot Interaction. My PhD research explores how robot navigation strategies influence human perception, comfort, and people’s willingness to work with robots in shared environments.

My work investigates how navigation behaviors can be shaped to support user comfort and willingness to engage with robots. By tuning robot motion to human expectations, I aim to enable smoother and more natural human–robot collaboration.

Beyond my core research, I am deeply interested in how movement cues can be improved to make robot behavior feel more intuitive, as well as in the role of safety-aware AI in robotics. I am passionate about finding ways to bridge the gap between academic research and real-world robotic systems that people can use and trust.

Research Experience

2022 – Present

Research Associate

Constructor University, Bremen, Germany

Working within the DFG SPP “The Active Self,” a project that investigates which behavioural cues lead people to attribute a “self” to artificial agents. Designing and running studies that manipulate robot navigation, movement cues, and behavioural characteristics such as speed, causality, equifinality, efficiency, social sensitivity, and attention sharing. Developing ROS-based multimodal pipelines to generate controlled robot behaviours and collect user responses. My work examines how these cues influence mind attribution, user trust, comfort, and people’s willingness to work with robots in shared environments.

Project Details
2020 – 2021

Researcher

University of Ljubljana, Slovenia

Studied how robot speed and end-effector type (safe vs. unsafe tools, e.g., sponge vs. blade-like tool) influence user trust, perceived safety, and willingness to work with robots. In the first study, participants observed a robot performing random movements with different end-effectors and reported their comfort and how close they were willing to approach the robot. In the second study, participants collaborated with the robot on two tasks under varying speeds and tool conditions, evaluating their comfort and willingness to work alongside the robot. Contributed study reports and publications for the CROPS project.
